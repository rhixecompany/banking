# Examples: Real-World Workflow

## Example 1: Teresa Torres' Continuous Discovery Article

### Initial Request

"I'm writing about continuous discovery for product managers. It's a topic I care deeply about but it feels abstract to readers. Help me create an outline that lands with specific examples."

### Clarifying Questions (from me)

- Who's the target audience? (Product managers mid-career)
- What's the unique angle? (Moving from annual roadmaps to continuous learning)
- What's the goal? (Help PMs see continuous discovery as essential practice)
- Existing research? (Yes, customer interviews and my own data from 5 companies)
- Desired length? (~2000 words)
- Writing style? (Direct, practical, conversational with authority)

### Outline Created Together

```markdown
# Outline: Continuous Discovery as Core PM Practice

## Hook

- Opening: "I watched a PM ignore 15 customer interviews because they were already locked into a roadmap"
- Why reader should care: This is happening in your org right now

## The Problem (Section 1)

- Annual planning creates lock-in
- Customer reality changes monthly
- PMs make decisions with stale data
- Examples: [Teresa's research from 5 companies]

## What Continuous Discovery Actually Is (Section 2)

- Definition: Structured learning that informs every decision
- NOT just customer interviews
- System for rapid insight → action
- Framework: [Teresa's specific approach]

## How to Start (Section 3)

- First discovery session (concrete steps)
- Common mistakes (what NOT to do)
- Quick wins: [Specific examples]

## Measuring Impact (Section 4)

- Metrics that show continuous discovery is working
- How to know you're doing it right
- Company cases: [Data from her 5 companies]

## Conclusion

- This isn't optional anymore
- Call to action: "Start with just one team"
- Final thought: Why this matters

## Research Needed

- [ ] Format customer interview templates
- [ ] Compile metrics from 5 companies
- [ ] Find 2-3 external studies on discovery effectiveness
```

### After Outline Approval

Teresa wrote Section 1 (The Problem) → I provided feedback focusing on clarity and examples → She revised → Moved to Section 2.

**Feedback on Section 1:**

```
What Works Well ✓
- Opening story is gripping (immediate tension)
- Three examples of lock-in feel specific, not generic
- Your voice comes through (direct, slightly frustrated tone)

Suggestions:
- The stat "15 customer interviews ignored" — do you have source for how common this is?
  Could add credibility if cited. (Or remove if you want to keep it anecdotal)
- Transition to "Here's why this matters" feels abrupt. Consider:
  "This happens because [system reason]. Here's why it matters..."

Your choice: Keep it punchy or add system context?

Specific Edit:
Original: "This is happening in your org right now"
Suggested: "This is happening in your org right now, even if you don't see it"
Why: Slightly more compelling accusation; makes it personal

Ready to move to Section 2?
```

### Final Result

2000-word article that reads like Teresa, sounds authoritative, has solid customer data backing every claim, and lands with her target audience (product managers who already respect her thinking).

## Example 3: Hook Improvement Iterations

### Original Hook (Writer's First Draft)

> "Artificial intelligence is changing product management. In this article, I explore how product managers can adapt to this shift."

### My Analysis & Alternatives

```
Current Hook Analysis:
- What works: You've identified an important topic
- What could be stronger: It's generic (could apply to any trending topic)
- Emotional impact: Low; reader has no reason to keep reading

Three Alternative Approaches:

Option 1 (Data-Driven):
"I just reviewed AI tools used by product managers at 50 companies. 80%
were solving the wrong problem. Here's how to actually use AI effectively."

Why it works: Surprising stat (80% wrong) + promise of useful knowledge

---

Option 2 (Story-Based):
"Last month, I watched a PM spend three days analyzing user feedback.
A bot did the same analysis in 20 minutes. But the bot missed the insight that changed the roadmap."

Why it works: Concrete story shows AI's limitation + value of human judgment

---

Option 3 (Question-Based):
"How many hours is your team spending on work that AI could do in minutes?
And more importantly: on what are you NOT spending time because of it?"

Why it works: Makes reader think about opportunity cost, not just automation
```

### Writer's Choice

"I like option 2 (story) because it matches my voice, but the third sentence needs work. Let me revise: 'But the bot missed the insight that convinced the CEO to fund our moonshot.'"

**My Response:** "Better. Specific outcome matters. Ready to draft section 1?"

### Result

Hook that captures attention _and_ sounds like the writer (authentic voice + compelling).

## Example 4: Section Feedback in Action

### Writer's Draft: "How to Structure Your Discovery Process"

> "Product managers often struggle with discovery. Many teams don't have a systematic approach. This leads to missed opportunities and wasted time. A good discovery process should include interviews, surveys, and data analysis. You should do this monthly. You should also document what you learn."

### Feedback Provided

```
What Works Well ✓
- You're identifying a real problem that readers face
- Clear structure (problem → solution framework)

Suggestions for Improvement:

Clarity Issue:
"Many teams don't have a systematic approach" is vague. What's missing?
Is it: lack of time? lack of structure? lack of tools? lack of accountability?
Be specific: "Many teams conduct discovery ad-hoc, without rhythm or documentation."

Evidence Gap:
"A good discovery process should include interviews, surveys, and data analysis" —
This feels like a template. Does YOUR process include all three, or do you recommend adapting?
Better: "Our most effective process combines lightweight interviews (10-15 people) with
behavioral data analysis. Surveys are optional, depending on what you're learning."

Specificity Win:
"Monthly" is too vague. Better: "Every two weeks, or whenever you're making a decision that
affects your roadmap" (shows it's driven by needs, not calendar)

Specific Line Edit:
Original: "You should also document what you learn."
Suggested: "Document the insight (not the transcript). Three bullet points max: What surprised you?
What changed your thinking? What's the next question?"
Why: Specific enough for readers to actually do it

Ready to revise this section?
```

### Writer's Revision

> "Product managers often struggle with discovery because they're doing it ad-hoc, without rhythm or documentation. Every decision you make should be informed by recent customer learning. Here's the process I've used at [Company A] and [Company B]: lightweight interviews (10-15 people) + behavioral data analysis. Every two weeks, or whenever you're making a major decision, document the insight—just three bullets: What surprised you? What changed your thinking? What's the next question?"

**My Response:** "Better. You've added specificity, examples, and your voice. The framework is now actionable. Ready for section 3?"

## Pattern Recognition Across Examples

What makes these work:

1. **Clear outline before drafting** — Not all-at-once writing; structured thinking first
2. **Section-by-section feedback** — Early feedback prevents downstream mistakes
3. **Writer's voice throughout** — I suggest, they choose; final piece reads like them
4. **Specific, actionable feedback** — Not "good work" but "here's what works and why"
5. **Research serves the voice** — Sources amplify authority, don't override perspective
6. **Respect for deadlines** — Quick iteration, not perfectionism
7. **Learning as you write** — Patterns from section 1 → applied independently in section 3

All of these pieces work together to create writing that's both excellent and authentically theirs.
