# Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do

This document outlines critical mistakes to avoid when assisting with collaborative writing.

## NEVER: Rewrite the Writer's Voice

**The Anti-Pattern**: You rewrite entire sections in your own prose or a "better" writing style.

**Why It Fails**:

- Kills the partnership; writer becomes editor of your draft, not co-creator
- Generic corporate prose replaces authentic voice
- Writer loses ownership of their own work
- You've just created a template, not collaboration

**What to Do Instead**:

- Make surgical line edits with alternatives, not wholesale rewrites
- Suggest improvements that preserve their word choice and rhythm
- Say: "Here's an alternative for clarity" not "Here's how to fix this"
- When major revision is needed: offer 2-3 options, let writer choose

---

## NEVER: Give Vague Praise Without Actionable Feedback

**The Anti-Pattern**: "Great work!" or "This section is good" or "I like this."

**Why It Fails**:

- Provides zero guidance for iteration
- Writer doesn't know what worked or why
- Wastes time (praise + confusion = no progress)
- Blocks the collaborative feedback loop

**What to Do Instead**:

- Name the specific strength: "The concrete example with the Slack notification makes this real"
- Tie to impact: "This example shows why it matters, not just that it matters"
- Balance with specific improvement: "Strength: specific example. Improvement: needs citation for the stat"
- Every comment answers: "What worked? Why? What's next?"

---

## NEVER: Skip Section-by-Section Iteration for Speed

**The Anti-Pattern**: Telling writer to draft everything, then doing one comprehensive review.

**Why It Fails**:

- Writer internalizes bad patterns before feedback arrives
- Analysis paralysis on large-scale comments
- Fixes from section 1 don't improve sections 3-5 (no course correction)
- Writer is emotionally invested in full draft; big revisions feel threatening

**What to Do Instead**:

- One section → feedback → revision → next section
- Early feedback prevents downstream mistakes
- Writer builds confidence incrementally
- Collaboration feels real (not "drop draft, await judgment")
- Feedback is digestible (not overwhelming)

---

## NEVER: Push Research Claims Without Verifying Credibility

**The Anti-Pattern**: You add citations to claims without checking source reputation or recency.

**Why It Fails**:

- Undermines writer credibility if citations are weak or outdated
- Published work with bad citations damages author reputation
- "I cited it because you said so" doesn't hold in peer review
- Shows you don't care about research rigor (contradicts skill purpose)

**What to Do Instead**:

- Verify source domain/author before suggesting citation
- Flag questionable sources: "This stat is from 2019; should we look for more recent data?"
- Explain why a source is good: "This is from McKinsey Global Institute (high reputation)"
- If unsure: "I found this, but please verify the original source"
- Suggest research paths, not assumed answers

---

## NEVER: Format Citations Inconsistently

**The Anti-Pattern**: Some citations are inline, others numbered, others footnote-style in the same document.

**Why It Fails**:

- Looks unprofessional and careless
- Makes documents hard to navigate
- When published, editorial teams have to standardize (extra work)
- Shows lack of attention to citation hygiene (contradicts skill promise)

**What to Do Instead**:

- Ask writer: "Which citation style do you prefer? Inline, numbered, or footnotes?"
- Maintain consistency throughout the entire document
- Use the same format in research.md as in final draft.md
- If transitioning styles: "You've used inline citations through section 3; should we convert 1-2 to numbered for the glossary?"

---

## NEVER: Suggest All-or-Nothing Rewrites

**The Anti-Pattern**: "Delete this section and rewrite it as..." or "This doesn't work; try this instead."

**Why It Fails**:

- Feels directive, not collaborative
- Writer loses autonomy and trust
- Dismisses their effort without acknowledging what worked
- Creates confrontation instead of partnership

**What to Do Instead**:

- Offer 2-3 options with reasoning for each
- Preserve elements that work: "The story is good; the transition to data is the gap"
- Suggest edits, not replacements: "Add this data point between these two paragraphs"
- Frame as alternatives: "Here's one way... another approach would be..."

---

## NEVER: Assume Outline is Final

**The Anti-Pattern**: Writer approves outline, but mid-way through drafting, changes structure without revisiting outline.

**Why It Fails**:

- Creates inconsistency between outline and draft
- Writer loses sight of original logic
- Feedback becomes disconnected from plan
- "I thought we covered this" confusion

**What to Do Instead**:

- Before draft progresses beyond 20%: "Are we still following the outline, or does the draft suggest changes?"
- If structure changes: "Update outline first, then draft. This keeps us aligned."
- After each section: "Does this match the outline, or should we update the plan?"

---

## NEVER: Ignore Writer's Preferred Feedback Style

**The Anti-Pattern**: You give detailed line-by-line feedback when writer wants high-level assessment, or vice versa.

**Why It Fails**:

- Mismatched feedback style frustrates writer
- Cognitive load mismatch (too much detail or not enough)
- Partnership breaks down if writer doesn't get what they need

**What to Do Instead**:

- Ask early: "Do you want detailed line edits or high-level structure feedback?"
- Match style to request: "Line-by-line? Or focus on flow?"
- Adapt if feedback style isn't working: "Is this level of detail helpful, or should I zoom out?"

---

## NEVER: Conflate Research with Writing

**The Anti-Pattern**: You conduct deep research before writer has outlined, or spend hours finding perfect sources for draft sections that might change.

**Why It Fails**:

- Wasted research on sections that get cut or restructured
- Writer doesn't learn research strategy (skill should teach, not do all work)
- Research might be too specialized/narrow if done before full outline
- Writer feels removed from research process (ownership loss)

**What to Do Instead**:

- Outline first, then identify research needs together
- Ask: "What do we still need to understand?" (collaborative decision)
- Show research paths, let writer explore some
- Research fills specific outline gaps, not "everything about topic"

---

## NEVER: Provide Feedback in a Vacuum Without Asking for Writer's Concerns

**The Anti-Pattern**: You review section and deliver feedback without asking what the writer is worried about.

**Why It Fails**:

- Your feedback might miss what writer is actually concerned about
- Writer feels unheard (you didn't ask, you just told)
- Feedback misses the forest for the trees

**What to Do Instead**:

- Before feedback: "Anything you're worried about in this section?"
- Listen for writer's concerns first
- Address those concerns in feedback
- Then add observations they didn't mention
- Feedback feels collaborative, not judged

---

**Summary**: These anti-patterns destroy the collaborative partnership that makes this skill valuable. Avoid them religiously.
