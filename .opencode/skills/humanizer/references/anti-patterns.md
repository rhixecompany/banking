# Anti-Patterns Checklist: What NOT to Do

**Use this quick reference when uncertain about what makes writing sound soulless or AI-generated.**

---

## NEVER DO: Soulless Writing Patterns

- **NEVER write every sentence the same length** — Humans naturally vary rhythm. Monotonous rhythm signals algorithmic output.
- **NEVER report facts neutrally without opinions** — Real humans react to information. Pure neutrality feels robotic.
- **NEVER avoid acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings** — Humans have nuanced thoughts. "I'm not sure about this" is authentic.
- **NEVER skip first-person perspective when appropriate** — "I think..." or "Here's what I notice..." signals a real person.
- **NEVER avoid personality, humor, or edge** — Safe writing without voice feels impersonal.
- **NEVER read like a Wikipedia article or press release** — Encyclopedia tone is distinctly non-human.

---

## NEVER DO: Content Inflation

- **NEVER use "marks a pivotal moment" or similar phrases** — Facts don't need inflated importance. Just state them.
- **NEVER claim something "contributes to broader trends"** — Support this with data or remove it.
- **NEVER list media outlets without explaining relevance** — Notability should be demonstrated, not asserted.
- **NEVER use multiple "-ing" phrases in one sentence** — One descriptive "-ing" is fine; two+ signal artificial depth.
- **NEVER attribute opinions to vague authorities** — "Experts say..." is not a source. Name someone or cite research.
- **NEVER include formulaic "Challenges and Future Prospects" sections** — Add new information or remove the section.

---

## NEVER DO: Vocabulary and Grammar

- **NEVER use AI vocabulary words in clusters** — Using 3+ of these in one paragraph: additionally, pivotal, landscape, testament, underscore, enhance, fostering → Signals LLM authorship
- **NEVER use "serves as" where "is" would work** — "The gallery is X" is clearer than "The gallery serves as X"
- **NEVER use "Not only...but..." or "It's not just...it's..."** — These constructions are overused. State ideas directly.
- **NEVER force ideas into groups of exactly three** — Let lists be 1, 2, 4, or 5 items if that's natural.
- **NEVER use four or more different terms for the same concept** — protagonist/character/hero/figure in one passage → Use one term consistently.
- **NEVER use "from X to Y" when X and Y aren't on a continuous scale** — "From Shakespeare to modern theater" is vague; list them separately.

---

## NEVER DO: Style and Formatting

- **NEVER use 2+ em dashes in a paragraph** — Em dashes add emphasis when used sparingly. Multiple per paragraph signals sales-copy AI style.
- **NEVER bold more than 1-2 terms per section** — Bold should highlight key concepts, not decorate random phrases.
- **NEVER use **Bold Label:** format for list items** — Creates artificial structure. Use prose or simple bullets instead.
- **NEVER use Title Case for all headings** — Use sentence case (capitalize first word only) in modern professional writing.
- **NEVER add emojis to formal headings or sections** — Emojis are rare in serious writing.
- **NEVER use curly quotation marks in technical writing** — Use straight quotes ("...") for consistency and compatibility.

---

## NEVER DO: Chatbot Artifacts

- **NEVER leave "I hope this helps!" or "Let me know if..."** — These mark chatbot correspondence, not content.
- **NEVER include knowledge-cutoff disclaimers** — "As of my last training update..." belongs in conversation, not in articles.
- **NEVER use sycophantic tone** — Excessive "Great question!" and "You're absolutely right!" are chatbot signatures.
- **NEVER leave "Certainly!" or "Of course!" in content** — These are conversational markers, not prose.

---

## NEVER DO: Wordiness and Hedging

- **NEVER use "In order to" when "To" works** — Remove filler phrases.
- **NEVER qualify every statement** — "It could potentially possibly be argued that..." → "The policy may..." is sufficient.
- **NEVER end with vague positive conclusions** — "Exciting times ahead!" adds nothing. End with concrete next steps or specific findings.
- **NEVER use 5+ hedging words per paragraph** — Could, might, may, possibly, apparently, arguably, somewhat, fairly, perhaps, it seems, tends to
  - These are legitimate in moderation
  - Excessive hedging signals uncertain AI output

---

## Pattern Combinations That Signal AI Writing

When you see these patterns together, you're likely looking at AI output:

1. **Pattern 7 + 13**: AI vocabulary words ("landscape", "pivotal") mixed with em dash overuse
2. **Pattern 4 + 10**: Promotional words ("vibrant", "stunning") organized into rule-of-three lists
3. **Pattern 1 + 3**: Inflated significance claims ("marks a moment") with multiple "-ing" descriptions
4. **Pattern 6 + 8**: Formulaic "Challenges" section using "serves as" instead of "is"
5. **Pattern 19 + 20**: "I hope this helps!" alongside "as of my last training update"

If you see 2+ of these combinations, the text is almost certainly AI-generated.

---

## Quick Self-Check: Is This AI Writing?

Answer these questions:

- [ ] Does it sound like a travel brochure or Wikipedia article?
- [ ] Are there 3+ em dashes per paragraph?
- [ ] Does it use 3+ of these words: pivotal, landscape, testament, underscore, enhance, moreover?
- [ ] Are ideas forced into groups of exactly three?
- [ ] Does it apologize for incomplete information?
- [ ] Does it thank you for attention ("I hope this helps!")?
- [ ] Does every sentence have similar length and structure?
- [ ] Are opinions entirely absent?
- [ ] Are sources vague ("experts say")?
- [ ] Does it have a formulaic "Challenges" section?

**If you answered YES to 3+:** This is likely AI-generated. Use the patterns reference to identify and fix.
