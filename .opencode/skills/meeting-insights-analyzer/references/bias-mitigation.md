# Bias Mitigation Framework

This file teaches you to recognize observer bias — the tendency to see what you expect to see. Read this BEFORE any analysis.

## The Observer Bias Problem

When you analyze your own meetings, your brain has a hypothesis about yourself:

- "I avoid conflict" → Brain finds hedging language everywhere
- "I'm a good listener" → Brain highlights questions you asked, ignores times you interrupted
- "I dominate meetings" → Brain counts your speaking time but dismisses context (were you facilitating?)

This is confirmation bias. Your observations become self-fulfilling prophecies.

## Three-Check System: Before You Conclude

Before reporting a pattern, run this mental checklist:

### Check 1: Opposite Hypothesis

State your finding: "I avoid conflict in meetings."

Now imagine the opposite: "I face conflict directly."

Scan your transcripts looking for examples that support the OPPOSITE hypothesis:

- Moments where you named a problem directly
- Times you didn't hedge or deflect
- Situations where you raised tension intentionally

Count these. If you find as many counterexamples as examples, the pattern is weaker than you thought.

**Goal**: Find genuine counterexamples (not just silence = agreement). If you can't find any, you're probably hunting selectively.

### Check 2: Context Variation

Even strong patterns have context limits. State your finding narrowly:

**Weak**: "I avoid conflict." **Stronger**: "I avoid direct feedback in one-on-one salary discussions, but I'm direct in peer planning meetings."

For your pattern, ask:

- Does it appear in ALL contexts, or specific ones?
- Does the context explain the behavior better than personality?
- What's different about the situations where it doesn't appear?

**Example**: "In hierarchical 1:1s (power imbalance), I use hedging language. In peer meetings (equal power), I'm direct."

This isn't a personality flaw; it's situational adaptation. Report the context.

### Check 3: Confounding Variables

List all alternative explanations:

**Pattern claim**: "I interrupt frequently, which shows dominance."

**Alternative explanations**:

- You think out loud and interrupt yourself, not others
- The meeting was fast-paced with overlapping speakers (medium effect, not personality)
- You're the facilitator and naturally speak more (role effect)
- This was one stressful meeting; in routine meetings you don't interrupt

Count interruptions BY others of you. Count self-interruptions separately. This clarifies the pattern.

## Confirmation Bias Signatures

If you notice these thoughts, STOP and re-examine:

1. **"This is obvious, I don't need to double-check"**
   - Red flag: You've stopped thinking critically
   - Action: Force yourself to find counterexamples; if you can't, you're selectively scanning

2. **"The evidence clearly shows..."**
   - Red flag: You're already at a conclusion; now hunting for support
   - Action: State the opposite conclusion and scan for support equally hard

3. **"All my examples fit the pattern perfectly"**
   - Red flag: Real patterns have some messiness; too-clean patterns are artifacts
   - Action: Look for the exceptions that should exist

4. **"I remember this happening a lot"**
   - Red flag: Memory is selective; you remember surprising instances, not statistical frequency
   - Action: Count actual instances. Frequency will probably be lower than your impression

5. **"Other people would agree with this pattern"**
   - Red flag: You're anchoring on what you think others think
   - Action: Ask them. Let their perception guide, not reinforce your hypothesis

## Structural Checks

### Sample Size Validation

Before claiming a pattern:

- [ ] Minimum 3 instances across 2+ meetings? (Or fewer meetings if very consistent behavior)
- [ ] Instances separated by time? (Not all in one week when you were stressed)
- [ ] Instances in different contexts? (Different people, meeting types, stress levels)

If not, state clearly: "Observed in limited contexts; insufficient for strong pattern claim."

### Frequency Sanity Check

Calculate actual percentage:

- You speak 60% of a 60-minute meeting = 36 minutes
- Is 36 minutes "dominating"? Only if others average 5 minutes. If they average 12 minutes, you're speaking 3x as much, which might be normal for a facilitator

The number alone means nothing. Compare to role and context.

### Timeline Validation

Don't mix signals from different periods:

- **Mistake**: "You avoid conflict" based on meetings from both calm Q1 and crisis-mode August
- **Better**: "In calm Q1, you facilitate collaboratively. In August crisis, you became directive."

Context changed; behavior adapted. That's not a personality trait; that's situational responding.

## Self-Compassion Check

Analysis is hardest when you're criticizing yourself. If you notice:

- Unusually harsh interpretation of your behavior
- Willingness to excuse same behavior in others
- Focusing only on flaws, not strengths
- Setting impossible standards

...pause. You're not being analytical; you're being self-punishing. Fair analysis acknowledges both strengths and areas for growth.

## The Gold Standard: Collaboration

The most reliable way to check bias: **get someone else's perspective**.

Ask a colleague: "In our meetings, when I avoid giving you feedback directly, what do you notice? Do I hedge?"

Their answer may surprise you. You might be hedging in your mind but coming across direct to them. Or vice versa.

Use outside perspective to calibrate your self-perception, not to confirm it.

## Applying These Checks in Practice

Before you write your report:

1. **State your pattern claim in plain language**
2. **Run Opposite Hypothesis check** — find genuine counterexamples
3. **Run Context Variation check** — narrow the claim to specific contexts
4. **Run Confounding Variables check** — list 3-5 alternative explanations
5. **Validate Sample Size** — do you have 3+ instances across 2+ contexts?
6. **Sanity-check Frequency** — do the numbers actually support the strength of claim?
7. **Calibrate with Outside Perspective** — if possible, check your interpretation against someone else's observation

If you pass all 7 checks, you have a genuine insight. If you skip checks because "it's obvious," you've found a bias, not a pattern.
