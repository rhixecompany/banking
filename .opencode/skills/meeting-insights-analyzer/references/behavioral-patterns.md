# Behavioral Pattern Analysis Guide

Load this file when analyzing specific communication patterns. Each section provides signals to watch, interpretation guidance, and caveats.

## Conflict Avoidance & Indirect Communication

### Signals to Observe

**Hedging language** (weakens directness):

- "Maybe", "kind of", "sort of", "I think", "potentially", "possibly", "arguably"
- Example: "Maybe we could, like, consider adjusting the timeline?"
- vs. "The timeline needs adjustment. What's blocking us?"

**Deflection** (avoids taking responsibility):

- "Whatever you think", "I'm open to anything", "Whatever works for you"
- "You're the expert", "Your call entirely"
- Example: "I was thinking we could accelerate delivery... but whatever you think is best!"
  - Suggests something important, then immediately backs off
  - Listener is left uncertain about what you actually need

**Subject changes** (when tension arises):

- Monitor meeting flow: Does the topic shift immediately when disagreement appears?
- Restarts with "Anyway...", "So...", "Moving on..."
- Note: May indicate discomfort OR efficient meeting management (context matters)

**Indirect requests** (hints instead of clear asks):

- "Maybe we should look at..." vs. "I need you to..."
- "I wonder if..." vs. "Can you..."
- "Do you think it's possible..." vs. "Will you..."

**Over-agreement** without clarity (agreeing to appease):

- "Yeah, but..." — appears to agree, then undercuts
- "I hear you, AND..." — acknowledges without committing
- "That's a good point, but I was thinking..." — dismisses while pretending to validate

**Not naming problems** (silence instead of speaking):

- Problem is discussed around but never named directly
- You stay silent while others discuss issue
- Caution: Silence might be good listening, not conflict avoidance — check for later questions/input

### Interpretation Caveats

**This doesn't mean**: You avoid conflict in all situations. Conflict avoidance is often contextual:

- **Hierarchy effect**: More avoidance in conversations with power imbalance (with boss, with direct reports) than peer conversations
- **Relationship effect**: More avoidance with people who have reacted poorly to feedback before
- **Stress effect**: More hedging under time pressure or when emotionally activated
- **Meeting type effect**: More hedging in large groups, more directness in 1:1s

**Report carefully**: "In salary discussions and feedback conversations, you use hedging language. In peer strategy meetings, you're direct. This suggests situational adaptation to power dynamics, not general conflict avoidance."

**Positive counterpart**: Look for moments where you DID name issues directly. These show you're capable of directness; avoidance is choice, not incapacity.

## Speaking Patterns & Speaking Ratio

### Signals to Observe

**Speaking percentage** (relative to meeting total):

- Calculate: Total minutes you spoke ÷ meeting length
- Example: You spoke 25 minutes in a 60-minute meeting = 41.7%
- IMPORTANT: Compare to role and meeting type, not to a universal standard

**Turn-taking consistency**:

- How many distinct speaking turns do you take?
- Are they evenly distributed or clustered (speak a lot early, quiet later)?
- Note: Fatigue or agenda might explain later silence, not disengagement

**Interruption patterns** (both by and of you):

- **By you of others**: Count moments where you start speaking while others are still talking
- **Of you by others**: How often do others interrupt you? (Shows power dynamics)
- **Of yourself**: Count moments you interrupt your own thoughts mid-sentence
  - High self-interruption + low interruption of others = thinking out loud, not dominance
  - High interruption of others + low self-interruption = dominant speech pattern

**Average turn length**:

- Measure average duration of speaking turns
- Longer turns might indicate: comfort with the room, deep thinking, or taking more air time
- Shorter turns might indicate: listening-focused or deferential

**Speaking lag** (time before you speak):

- How long into the meeting before you say your first thing?
- Do you jump in immediately or wait for others?
- Note: Some people are reflective and wait to understand before speaking (strength, not weakness)

### Interpretation Caveats

**Role matters enormously**:

- Facilitator/leader: Should speak 40-60% of meeting time for healthy facilitation
- Participant in others' meetings: Should speak 20-40% depending on expertise and seniority
- Expert contributor: Might speak 30-50% of technical discussions
- Listening role (coaching, counseling): Might speak only 10-20%

**Without role context, "You spoke 60%" is meaningless.** Always interpret relative to role.

**Medium effect**:

- In high-energy meetings, people naturally interrupt more and speak longer
- In formal settings, speech is more measured
- Video meetings change dynamics vs. in-person
- Large groups suppress participation; small groups increase it

**Don't confuse**:

- Speaking less = listening better. Listening quality is measured by follow-up questions and reference to others' points, not silence.
- Speaking more = dominance. Speaking ratio without considering role is incomplete data.

## Filler Words & Linguistic Uncertainty

### Signals to Observe

**Filler word inventory**:

- "Um", "uh", "like", "you know", "actually", "literally"
- "Kind of", "sort of", "I guess", "maybe", "I think"
- "Right?", "Yeah?", ending statements like questions

**Frequency measurement**:

- Count per minute of speaking
- Compare early-meeting (fresh) vs. late-meeting (tired)
- Compare high-stakes discussion vs. routine discussion

**Contextual increase**:

- Does filler word frequency spike when discussing difficult topics?
- When you're uncertain vs. confident?
- When answering unexpected questions vs. prepared topics?

**Pattern**: Look for correlation between filler words and emotional activation (nervousness, disagreement, uncertainty)

### Interpretation Caveats

**Cultural and generational factors override personality**:

- "Like" is standard Gen Z speech; not a sign of uncertainty
- "Actually" is common in certain professional subcultures (academia, tech)
- Different regions have different fillers (Australian English: "eh?"; American Southern English: "you know")

**Don't interpret as**: Lacking confidence or intelligence. Filler words are production pauses while thinking; they have no correlation to actual knowledge.

**More interesting pattern**: Does filler word _increase_ during stressful moments?

- "I think we should... um... maybe... consider the timeline" (uncertain, hedging)
- vs. "We should accelerate delivery because the customer needs it by Q4" (clear, no fillers)

This reveals when YOU become uncertain, not whether you're generally uncertain.

**Positive aspect**: Low filler words might indicate over-preparation or script-reading, not necessarily good communication. Natural speech has some fillers.

## Active Listening & Engagement

### Signals to Observe

**References to others' points**:

- "As you mentioned earlier..."
- "Building on what Sarah said..."
- "When you described the timeline constraint..."
- Shows you tracked and retained what others said

**Clarifying questions**:

- Questions that dig deeper into others' ideas
- "Can you help me understand..." vs. "Why are you wrong?"
- Count questions directed at understanding vs. challenging

**Paraphrasing**:

- "So what I'm hearing is..." followed by accurate summary
- Shows you processed, not just heard
- Caution: Might be performance of listening vs. actual understanding — check if paraphrase is accurate

**Building on ideas**:

- "Your idea about X, combined with our constraint on Y, suggests we could Z"
- Shows synthesis, not just acknowledgment

**Asking for input from quiet people**:

- "I haven't heard from X yet — what do you think?"
- Shows awareness of group dynamics
- Indicates inclusive facilitation

**Response patterns**:

- Do you respond to what others said, or move immediately to your point?
- Do you acknowledge ideas before stating disagreement?

### Interpretation Caveats

**Don't confuse listening with speaking less**:

- An active listener might ask many questions (high speaking time) while being deeply engaged
- A quiet person might be reflective or disengaged — context determines which

**Listening quality is about what you do when you DO speak**:

- Do you reference what others said?
- Do you ask clarifying questions?
- Do you build on ideas or pivot?

**Asking many questions might indicate**:

- Genuine curiosity (strength)
- OR lack of confidence in your own ideas (developmental area)
- Context shows which

## Leadership Style & Decision-Making

### Signals to Observe

**Directive vs. Collaborative**:

_Directive_:

- Statements vs. questions (you tell, don't ask)
- "Here's what we're going to do" vs. "What should we do?"
- Makes decisions without consensus
- Examples: "The timeline is two weeks. Let's move.", "We're canceling the feature."

_Collaborative_:

- Questions vs. statements (you ask, don't tell)
- "What do you all think about..." vs. "Here's what we should..."
- Seeks input before deciding
- Builds group consensus
- Examples: "How should we approach this?", "What are your concerns?"

**Handling disagreement**:

- Does disagreement escalate or de-escalate?
- Do you validate others' points before explaining yours?
- Do you change your mind when presented with new evidence?
- Do you shut down dissent or encourage it?

**Inclusion of quiet voices**:

- Do you notice when someone hasn't spoken?
- Do you specifically invite them in?
- Or assume silence means agreement/understanding?

**Clarity of decisions**:

- After discussion, is the decision clear?
- Do action items have owners and deadlines?
- Or does the meeting end with ambiguity?

**Pace of decision-making**:

- Do you decide quickly and move forward?
- Do you extend discussion too long, seeking unnecessary consensus?
- Context determines if this is efficiency or indecision

### Interpretation Caveats

**Directive ≠ Bad, Collaborative ≠ Good**:

- Directive is appropriate for crisis moments, clear expertise, or urgent decisions
- Collaborative is appropriate for decisions affecting team, complex problems, or relationship-building
- Best leaders shift between styles based on context

**Power dynamics matter**:

- You might be collaborative with peers but directive with direct reports (or vice versa)
- This is often healthy adaptation, not inconsistency

**Meeting format affects leadership visibility**:

- Large group meetings: Harder to facilitate inclusively; might look more directive
- Small team meetings: Can facilitate more collaboratively
- 1:1 meetings: Dynamics very different from group settings

**Apparent indecision might be**:

- Genuine consensus-building (strength)
- OR inability to make decisions (development area)
- Context of urgency and complexity determines which

## Next Steps

After identifying a pattern in one of these areas:

1. **Validate minimum 3 instances** across 2+ meetings before claiming pattern
2. **Check context variation** — does pattern hold in all contexts or specific ones?
3. **Look for counterexamples** — moments when you didn't exhibit this pattern
4. **Name confounding variables** — what else might explain this behavior?
5. **Report with caveats** — "In [context], you [behavior]. This might indicate [interpretation], especially given [context], though it could also reflect [alternative explanation]."

The most honest analysis acknowledges complexity, context, and multiple interpretations.
