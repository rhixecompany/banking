# Edge Cases & Ambiguous Situations

This file handles difficult analysis scenarios where standard guidance doesn't directly apply.

## Missing Speaker Labels or Labels are Ambiguous

### The Problem

Transcript like:

```
00:00:14 Speaker 1: "I think we should move the launch forward."
00:00:22 Speaker 2: "That timeline seems aggressive."
```

No context for who Speaker 1 and Speaker 2 are. You can't analyze your own patterns without knowing which speaker is you.

### What to Do

**Step 1: Ask the user for context**

"I see the transcript has speaker labels but no names. Can you tell me:

- Who was each speaker?
- Who were you in this meeting?
- Or: provide a labeled transcript?"

**Step 2: If context is provided, analyze with clear limitations note**

"Analysis Note: Speaker identification provided by user. If speaker labels were inaccurate, pattern conclusions may be wrong."

**Step 3: If speaker context is unavailable**

"Unable to analyze individual patterns without speaker identification. I can provide:

- Team-level patterns (meeting energy, disagreement dynamics, question frequency)
- Transcript structure analysis (how the group functioned)
- But NOT personal communication patterns

To enable personal analysis, please provide a transcript with clear speaker labels or names."

## Contradictory Patterns: "You Interrupt Frequently AND Ask a Lot of Questions"

### The Problem

You find:

- 12 interruptions in one meeting
- 18 clarifying questions
- Conclusion: "You're domineering yet curious" (seems contradictory)

### Analysis Strategy

**Step 1: Separate self-interruptions from other-interruptions**

- Count interruptions of YOUR OWN speech (you start talking, don't finish, keep going)
- Count interruptions of OTHERS' speech (you cut them off)

Often, high interrupt count is mostly self-interruption (thinking out loud) with few actual interruptions of others.

**Report**: "12 total interruptions: 8 were self-interruptions (incomplete thoughts you finished), 4 were interruptions of others' speech. Combined with 18 clarifying questions, pattern shows: you think out loud but when others speak, you're deeply curious and listen for detail."

**Step 2: Analyze timing and context**

When do interruptions occur?

- During YOUR explanations → self-interruption (thinking out loud)
- During others' explanations → dominating pattern

When do questions occur?

- When you want to understand → active listening
- When you're challenging → aggressive questioning

These may be different patterns in different contexts, not a contradiction.

**Step 3: Report the contextual variation**

"Paradox resolved: In explaining your ideas, you think out loud and interrupt yourself frequently. When others are explaining, you shift into deep listening mode with many clarifying questions. This is actually good facilitation — you're making space for thinking while also staying curious about others' contributions."

## No Clear Patterns Emerge

### The Problem

You analyzed 4 meetings looking for conflict avoidance, speaking patterns, active listening, leadership style — and found... relatively stable, consistent communication.

No dramatic hedging, no extreme speaking ratios, no obvious avoidance, no dominance.

**Temptation**: Report "no significant findings" and stop.

**Reality**: This IS a significant finding. It's a strength profile, not a failure to analyze.

### What to Do

**Step 1: Reframe the lack of extremes as stability**

"No conflict avoidance patterns detected. This suggests either:

1. You naturally communicate directly when addressing issues
2. You navigate these meetings without tension (may indicate good relationship dynamics)
3. Insufficient conflict moments in these meetings to trigger avoidance patterns

Recommend: Reanalyze a meeting that you know involved disagreement."

**Step 2: Shift to positive pattern identification**

Instead of looking for things you DON'T do, look for what you DO do:

- "Moments of active listening" (questions that build on others' ideas)
- "Clear communication" (few hedging words, direct statements)
- "Inclusive facilitation" (inviting quieter people in)
- "Collaborative decision-making" (seeking input before deciding)

**Report**: "Across 4 meetings, no conflict avoidance detected. Positive patterns observed:

- Asks clarifying questions consistently (average 6 per meeting)
- Invites input from team members by name
- Follows disagreements with direct acknowledgment
- Completes action items with clear owners"

**Step 3: Offer growth edge**

Even stability can have developmental areas:

- "Could you be more willing to take bold stances instead of always seeking consensus?"
- "Could you facilitate faster in crisis moments, or do you naturally prefer collaborative?
- "Are there contexts where you COULD be more direct?"

This acknowledges strength while pointing to growth.

## Very Long Transcripts (10+ Hour Meetings, Marathon Sessions)

### The Problem

Analyzing a 12-hour working session or all-hands meeting where patterns may shift dramatically over time.

### What to Do

**Step 1: Segment by time or topic**

"This is a 12-hour meeting. I'll segment by hour (or by topic) and analyze patterns separately to account for fatigue effects."

**Step 2: Track fatigue and engagement changes**

- Speaking ratio hour 1 vs. hour 12 (likely drops as fatigue sets in)
- Filler words increase or decrease?
- Question frequency changes?
- Interruption patterns change?

**Report**: "Early meeting (hours 1-3): You're highly engaged, ask detailed questions, facilitate collaboratively. Late meeting (hours 10-12): You become quieter, shorter contributions, defer more to others. This pattern suggests fatigue-driven disengagement."

**Step 3: Provide composite pattern**

- "Overall, you show [pattern]"
- "With time-of-day effect: [early pattern] that shifts to [late pattern]"
- "This is normal fatigue response, not personality change"

## Minimal Data (Only 1-2 Meetings)

### The Problem

User says "I only have 2 meetings transcribed. Can you analyze my patterns?"

Patterns require data across different contexts. One or two meetings is insufficient.

### What to Do

**Clearly state the limitation**:

"Analysis with only 2 meetings has significant limits:

- Can't distinguish stable patterns from temporary effects
- Context-specific behaviors might look like personality traits
- One stressful meeting might be anomaly, not pattern

Recommendation: Collect at least 2 additional meetings (4+ total) for reliable pattern analysis."

**But provide value anyway**:

"From these 2 meetings, I can note specific observations:

- Meeting 1: You [behavior] when [context]. This suggests [possible pattern], but needs validation across more meetings.
- Meeting 2: You [different behavior]. Possible explanations: [context variation] or [situational adaptation].

For confirmed patterns, please provide 2+ additional meetings."

## Conflicting Evidence: Pattern in Some Meetings, Not Others

### The Problem

"You avoid conflict in meetings with your boss, but not in peer meetings" — the pattern is real, but it's contextual.

Not a contradiction; it's situational adaptation.

### What to Do

**Validate the context specificity**:

- Boss meetings (power imbalance) → More hedging
- Peer meetings (equal power) → More directness
- Team meetings (responsibility) → More collaborative

**Report the context variation explicitly**:

"Conflict avoidance is situational, not personality-based:

- In 1:1s with your boss: You use hedging language (maybe, kind of)
- In peer strategy meetings: You're direct and name issues clearly
- In team meetings: You facilitate collaboratively, encourage disagreement

This suggests you're sensitive to power dynamics and adapt your communication appropriately. It's not a flaw; it's awareness of relational context."

**Frame positively**:

"Rather than 'conflict avoidance,' reframe as 'contextual communication adaptation.' You calibrate your directness based on the relationship and power dynamic. This is skillful."

## Analysis Reveals Patterns Contrary to User's Self-Belief

### The Problem

User says: "I dominate meetings."

But analysis shows: Relatively balanced speaking time, asks many questions, frequently defers to others.

### Approach with Care

**Don't immediately challenge their self-perception**:

Instead, present evidence factually:

"You mentioned dominating meetings. Here's what I observed:

- Speaking time: 35% of meeting (participant baseline, not dominant)
- Questions asked: 14 (high engagement, not dominance)
- Interruptions of others: 2 (low)
- Moments you suggested ideas: 4
- Moments you asked for others' input: 6

Pattern suggests you're collaborative and curious, not dominating.

Does this match what you expected to find? If not, possible explanations:

- You feel like you're dominating emotionally (took big stance on an issue)
- Memory is selective (remember the moments you spoke, not the silence)
- You're comparing to a baseline that's unrealistic
- You dominate in other meetings (these 4 might be atypical)

Let's dig deeper: Which meetings feel like you dominated? Let's analyze those specifically."

**Respect their reality while offering alternative frames**:

They may have accurate self-knowledge you're missing, OR they may have internalized false belief. Data helps clarify which.

## Next Steps

When you encounter an edge case:

1. **Name the specific problem** clearly
2. **State what analysis becomes possible** (and what becomes impossible)
3. **Offer fallback approach** (what CAN you analyze, even with limitation?)
4. **Ask clarifying questions** to the user for additional data
5. **Report with explicit caveats** about what this analysis doesn't cover

Honest analysis acknowledges its own limitations.
