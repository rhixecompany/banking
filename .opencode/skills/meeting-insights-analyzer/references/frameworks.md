# Analysis Methodology & Frameworks

This file describes HOW to conduct rigorous behavioral analysis — the meta-framework that ties everything together.

## The Hypothesis-Observation-Validation Cycle

Good analysis follows this cycle repeatedly:

### Phase 1: Hypothesis Formation

Start with a question, not a conclusion:

- "What are my conflict avoidance patterns?" (question, not hypothesis)
- Hypothesis: "I likely avoid conflict in status-imbalanced meetings"

Keep the hypothesis exploratory, not confirmatory. You're investigating, not proving.

### Phase 2: Observation

Now look at data with the hypothesis in mind, BUT also look for counterexamples:

- Scan for instances that SUPPORT the hypothesis (conflict avoidance behaviors)
- Scan equally hard for instances that CONTRADICT it (direct feedback, named problems)
- Note context for each instance (who was present, what was discussed, your emotional state)

**Critical**: Don't stop when you find 3 supporting examples. Keep scanning until you have a sense of:

- How frequent is this behavior?
- In what contexts does it appear?
- When does it NOT appear?

### Phase 3: Validation

Before reporting, test the pattern:

- **Temporal test**: Are instances spread across different meetings, or clustered in one week?
- **Context test**: Do they all share context (e.g., all with your boss)? If so, is that context driving the pattern?
- **Contradiction test**: Find the strongest counterexample. How does it relate to your hypothesis?
- **Alternative explanation test**: What else could explain these observations?

### Phase 4: Refined Hypothesis

Restate the pattern with specificity:

**Weak**: "I avoid conflict"

**Strong**: "In meetings with my manager (power imbalance), I use hedging language when discussing schedule concerns. In peer meetings, I name concerns directly. This suggests contextual adaptation to power dynamics."

The refined hypothesis is narrower, more specific, and accounts for variation.

## Five Levels of Evidence Strength

When you claim a pattern, calibrate how strong your evidence is:

### Level 1: Single Observation (Weakest)

"In one meeting, you hedged when giving feedback."

Strength: Very low. Could be one-off, could be tiredness, could be that particular relationship.

Statement: "Observed one instance..." Never claim pattern.

### Level 2: Repeated Observation in One Context

"In your last 3 meetings with your boss, you used hedging language."

Strength: Low-moderate. Pattern is real in this context, but generalizability unknown.

Statement: "In meetings with authority figures, you consistently hedge..."

### Level 3: Pattern Across Contexts with Context Variation

"You hedge with your boss (all 3 meetings). You don't hedge with peers (clear in 4 peer meetings). You hedge moderately in large groups (5 team meetings)."

Strength: Moderate-high. Clear pattern with clear context variation.

Statement: "Conflict avoidance is contextual. Stronger in hierarchical relationships, weaker in peer relationships..."

### Level 4: Pattern with Counterexample Explanation

"You hedge in 8 out of 10 authority-relationship meetings. The 2 exceptions: (1) scheduled feedback conversation where you prepared, (2) crisis moment requiring directness. This suggests context + preparation matter."

Strength: High. You've accounted for variation and explained exceptions.

Statement: "General pattern with clear conditions: You hedge with authority figures, unless the situation is prepared-for or urgent..."

### Level 5: Pattern with Mechanism Understanding (Strongest)

"You hedge with authority figures due to perceived power imbalance. Evidence: You hedge more with your C-suite boss than with your peer-level manager. You hedge less in meetings where you establish expertise upfront. This suggests the pattern is driven by power perception, not general communication style."

Strength: Very high. You understand the mechanism, not just the pattern.

Statement: "Your conflict avoidance pattern appears driven by power dynamics perception. When you establish equal footing (expertise, preparation), directness increases..."

## Distinguishing Personality from Adaptation

This is the hardest analytical distinction.

**Personality trait**: You avoid conflict consistently across contexts, regardless of power dynamics or stakes.

**Adaptive behavior**: You avoid conflict in some contexts (high stakes, power imbalance) but not others (routine, peer-level).

### How to Distinguish

**Test 1: Context breadth**

If pattern appears across MANY contexts (with boss, with peers, with reports, with strangers), stronger evidence of personality.

If pattern appears in LIMITED contexts (only authority figures, only in public, only with certain people), stronger evidence of adaptation.

**Test 2: Preparation effect**

Does preparation change the pattern?

- If you prepare a difficult conversation with your boss and deliver it directly → Evidence of adaptation, not personality
- If you hedge even in prepared conversation → Evidence of personality trait

**Test 3: Motivation effect**

Does high-stakes/important conversation change the pattern?

- If you're direct when the stakes are high ("This project will fail if we don't change course NOW") → Evidence of adaptation
- If you hedge even in critical moments → Evidence of personality trait

**Test 4: Observer perception**

Does your self-perception match others' perception?

- If boss says "You seem conflict-avoiding" and peers say "You're direct" → Evidence of contextual adaptation
- If everyone agrees "You avoid conflict" → Evidence of personality trait
- Mismatch: Check your own bias (are you seeing avoidance where others see listening?)

**Reporting the distinction**:

- Personality: "You generally avoid conflict across situations"
- Adaptation: "You adapt your directness based on power dynamics. More hedging with authority figures; more directness with peers."

Adaptation is often a strength (emotional intelligence). Personality trait may need development.

## Analytical Pitfall: The Coherence Trap

Your brain wants narratives to cohere. It's uncomfortable to say "You're both conflict-avoiding AND direct."

So your brain forces coherence:

- "You're actually conflict-avoiding, and when you're direct, that's compensatory"
- "You're actually direct, and when you hedge, that's atypical tiredness"

### How to Spot This

If you're narrating: "Despite [contradiction], actually [single coherent story]" — you're forcing coherence.

Instead, embrace the contradiction:

- "You're direct in routine discussions"
- "You're hedging in high-stakes feedback"
- "These both coexist; context determines which shows up"

Humans are contextual, not monolithic.

## Building a Longitudinal Pattern (Comparison Over Time)

When analyzing the same person across multiple time periods (Q1 vs Q2, before coaching vs after):

### Setup

- [ ] Clear date boundaries (Q1 = Jan-Mar, Q2 = Apr-Jun)
- [ ] Similar number of meetings in each period (don't compare 3 Q1 meetings to 15 Q2 meetings)
- [ ] Similar meeting types in each period (if Q1 is board meetings and Q2 is 1:1s, comparison is invalid)

### Comparison Method

1. **Baseline pattern identification** (first period)
   - What is your communication pattern in Q1?

2. **Test period pattern identification** (second period)
   - What is your communication pattern in Q2?

3. **Comparison** (holding context constant)
   - In similar meeting types:
   - Speaking ratio changed from X% to Y%
   - Hedging language decreased from Z instances to W instances
   - Question frequency increased from A to B

4. **Causation careful inference**
   - You improved from Q1 to Q2 ✓ (observation)
   - You improved BECAUSE you had coaching (causation claim) ⚠️
   - Coaching may have helped, but also: you had more familiar team, fewer crises, more experience in role, etc.

**Safe statement**: "From Q1 to Q2, you increased directness in feedback conversations. This timeline aligns with your coaching engagement, though other factors may have contributed (more experience in role, team stability, etc.)."

## Strength vs. Development Area Framing

All patterns can be framed two ways:

**Same behavior, two frames**:

| Behavior | Growth Frame | Strength Frame |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ask many questions | Might indicate uncertainty, decision paralysis | Indicates curiosity, engagement, learning-oriented |
| Low speaking time | Might indicate disengagement, not contributing | Indicates thoughtful listening, letting others contribute |
| Direct communication | Might seem harsh or domineering | Indicates clarity, confidence, respect for others' time |
| Collaborative approach | Might indicate slowness, lack of decisiveness | Indicates emotional intelligence, team buy-in, inclusive |

**How to choose the right frame**:

1. **Ask: Does the behavior serve the person's goals?**
   - If goal is "be a better listener" → "You ask many questions" is a strength
   - If goal is "move faster" → "You ask many questions" might be development area

2. **Look at outcomes**:
   - Do others seem engaged? Do projects move forward? Do people trust you?
   - Outcomes validate the frame

3. **Check for hidden downsides**:
   - High directness can sometimes miss relational impact
   - High listening can sometimes seem passive
   - No behavior is purely strength; acknowledge nuance

**Best practice**: Present the behavior neutrally, then offer multiple frames:

"You ask many clarifying questions (avg 10 per meeting). This might reflect:

- _Strength frame_: Genuine curiosity and engagement with others' ideas
- _Development frame_: Possible preference for understanding before action, which can slow decision-making
- _Contextual_: In exploratory meetings, valuable; in time-constrained decisions, might slow things down

What's your experience — does this pattern serve you well?"

## The Self-Report Problem

You're analyzing your own meetings. This creates inevitable bias:

### Bias sources:

1. **Memory**: You remember striking moments more than statistical frequency
2. **Motivation**: You want to find certain patterns (prove you can improve, or prove you're already good)
3. **Shame/Pride**: Uncomfortable patterns get minimized; proud patterns get inflated
4. **Narrative coherence**: Your brain constructs a story, then finds evidence to fit it

### How to manage:

1. **Use transcripts, not memory**
   - Don't trust "I remember hedging a lot"
   - Count actual instances: 3 times vs. 12 times
   - Number often surprises you

2. **Analyze before concluding**
   - Look at the evidence FIRST
   - THEN draw conclusion
   - Not: Form conclusion, then look for supporting evidence

3. **Seek outside perspective**
   - Ask someone who was in the meeting: "Did I seem conflict-avoiding?"
   - Their perspective calibrates your self-perception
   - Don't debate it; listen for the signal

4. **Report with uncertainty**
   - "This is my analysis based on these transcripts. My own bias likely affects interpretation. A colleague's perspective would strengthen or challenge this conclusion."

## The Meta-Principle: Honest Analysis Requires Doubt

The best analysis sounds less certain, not more:

❌ "You clearly avoid conflict. The evidence overwhelmingly shows..."

✓ "You frequently hedge when discussing concerns with authority figures. This might indicate conflict avoidance, or might indicate contextual communication adaptation to power dynamics. The pattern is real; the interpretation is less certain."

The second is better analysis because it:

- Acknowledges what's clearly observed (hedging)
- Acknowledges interpretation uncertainty (what it means)
- Invites the person to test the interpretation against their own experience

Good behavioral analysis is humble about its conclusions while being precise about its observations.
